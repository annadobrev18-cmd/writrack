## The Shift in Big Oil's Strategy
After years of prioritizing share buybacks, Big Oil companies are once again focusing on drilling and exploration. This significant shift in strategy has major implications for energy investors and the industry as a whole.

### Historical Context
To understand the significance of this shift, it's essential to look at the historical context. In the early 2010s, the oil price crash led to a significant decline in oil companies' revenues and profits. In response, many Big Oil companies turned to share buybacks as a way to return value to shareholders and boost their stock prices. This strategy was successful in the short term, but it also meant that companies were not investing enough in new projects and exploration.

#### The Impact of Share Buybacks
The focus on share buybacks had several consequences. Firstly, it led to a decline in the oil industry's overall investment in new projects and exploration. According to a report by the International Energy Agency (IEA), the oil industry's investment in new projects and exploration declined by 40% between 2014 and 2017. This decline in investment has led to a shortage of new oil discoveries, which could have significant implications for the industry's long-term sustainability.

### Market Impact
The shift back to drilling and exploration is likely to have a significant impact on the market. As Big Oil companies increase their investment in new projects, it could lead to an increase in oil production and a subsequent decrease in oil prices. This could have a negative impact on the stock prices of oil companies, at least in the short term.
