The banking sector in Australia and New Zealand is facing increased regulatory pressure, with the Big Four banks - Commonwealth Bank, Westpac, National Australia Bank, and ANZ - being subjected to stricter oversight. This development has significant implications for the financial sector, with potential ripple effects on the global economy.

### Historical Context

The Big Four banks in Australia have long dominated the country's banking landscape, with a combined market share of over 80%. However, in recent years, they have faced criticism for their role in various financial scandals, including money laundering and rate-rigging. The Australian government has responded by introducing stricter regulations, including the Banking Executive Accountability Regime (BEAR), which holds senior executives accountable for misconduct.

### Regulatory Pressure

The latest regulatory pressure on the Big Four banks comes in the form of increased capital requirements and stricter lending standards. The Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) has announced plans to increase the capital requirements for the Big Four banks, citing concerns over their ability to withstand economic shocks. This move is expected to increase the banks' funding costs and reduce their profitability.

### Sector Rotation

The increased regulatory pressure on the Big Four banks is likely to lead to sector rotation, with investors shifting their focus to smaller banks and non-bank lenders. This trend is already evident, with smaller banks such as Macquarie Group and Bendigo and Adelaide Bank experiencing increased demand for their shares.

### Global Ripple Effects

The regulatory crackdown on the Big Four banks in Australia and New Zealand is likely to have global implications, with potential ripple effects on the financial sector. The increased capital requirements and stricter lending standards may lead to a reduction in lending, which could have a negative impact on economic growth.
