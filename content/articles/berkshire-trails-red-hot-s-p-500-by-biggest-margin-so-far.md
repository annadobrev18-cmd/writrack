## Berkshire's Uncharacteristic Underperformance
Berkshire Hathaway, the conglomerate led by legendary investor Warren Buffett, is trailing the S&P 500 by its largest margin so far in 2026. The company's B shares are running 16.3 percentage points behind the benchmark index year-to-date, a gap that has caught the attention of investors and market analysts alike. This underperformance is unusual for Berkshire, which has historically been a strong performer in the stock market.

### Historical Context: Berkshire's Past Performance
To understand the significance of Berkshire's current underperformance, it's essential to look at its historical performance. Over the past few decades, Berkshire has consistently outperformed the S&P 500, with its Class A shares delivering an average annual return of 20% since 1965. This impressive track record has earned Berkshire a loyal following among investors and has made it one of the most widely held and respected companies in the world.

#### Berkshire's Diversified Portfolio
One of the key factors behind Berkshire's historical success has been its diversified portfolio of businesses. The company has a vast array of subsidiaries, including insurance companies, retail businesses, and industrial manufacturers. This diversification has helped Berkshire to weather economic downturns and has provided a steady stream of earnings and cash flow. However, in recent years, the company's portfolio has become increasingly concentrated in a few key sectors, including technology and finance.

### Market Impact: Why is Berkshire Underperforming?
So, why is Berkshire underperforming the S&P 500 in 2026? There are several factors that may be contributing to this gap. One possible reason is the company's exposure to the technology sector, which has been experiencing a slowdown in recent months. Berkshire has significant investments in companies like Apple and Amazon, which have been affected by the downturn in the tech sector.

#### Interest Rates and Inflation
Another factor that may be impacting Berkshire's performance is the rise in interest rates and inflation. The company has a large portfolio of bonds and other fixed-income securities, which have been negatively impacted by the increase in interest rates. Additionally, higher inflation has increased the cost of goods and services for many of Berkshire's subsidiaries, which has put pressure on their profit margins.
