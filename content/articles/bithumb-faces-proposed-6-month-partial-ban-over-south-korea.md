### Background: South Korea's Crypto Regulatory Framework

South Korea has been at the forefront of cryptocurrency adoption, with a vibrant market that has seen significant growth over the years. However, this growth has also been accompanied by increased regulatory scrutiny. The country's Financial Services Commission (FSC) has been proactive in implementing and enforcing strict AML and Know-Your-Customer (KYC) regulations, aiming to prevent illicit activities such as money laundering and terrorist financing.

The FSC's regulatory framework for cryptocurrencies is built around several key pillars:
- **AML/KYC Compliance**: Exchanges are required to implement robust AML/KYC measures to ensure that all transactions are legitimate and that customers are properly verified.
- **Licensing Requirements**: Cryptocurrency exchanges must obtain a license from the FSC to operate legally in South Korea. This involves meeting specific capital requirements and demonstrating the ability to comply with AML/KYC regulations.
- **Transaction Monitoring**: Exchanges are expected to monitor all transactions for suspicious activity and report any anomalies to the authorities.

### Bithumb's Proposed Partial Ban: Implications and Analysis

The proposed 6-month partial ban on Bithumb is a significant development that underscores the FSC's commitment to enforcing AML regulations. The regulatory challenges faced by Bithumb are not isolated to South Korea; they reflect a broader trend of increased scrutiny of cryptocurrency businesses worldwide. #### Global Regulatory Landscape

The global regulatory landscape for cryptocurrencies is complex and evolving. While some countries have embraced cryptocurrencies and implemented clear, supportive regulations, others have taken a more cautious or even hostile approach.

- **USA**: The regulatory environment in the US is fragmented, with different agencies (SEC, CFTC, FinCEN) overseeing different aspects of the crypto space. Recent developments, such as the introduction of the Lummis-Gillibrand bill, aim to provide clearer guidelines for the industry.
- **EU**: The European Union has taken a more unified approach, with the implementation of the Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation. MiCA provides a comprehensive framework for the regulation of cryptocurrencies and related services across the EU.
